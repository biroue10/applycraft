10 Common Interview Questions and How to Answer Them in 2026

You landed the interview — well done, that's already a win. Now you have to nail it. The good news: most recruiters ask the same questions, and you can prepare for them in advance. This guide covers the 10 most common ones, how to answer each, real examples, and the mistakes that sink candidates.

The principle: prepare without reciting

A strong interview isn't a memorized monologue — it's a conversation where you know where you're going. Prepare your ideas, not a rigid script. The recruiter is really assessing three things: can you do the job, will you fit in, and are you motivated. Every answer should speak to one of these.

The 10 common questions and how to answer them

1. "Tell me about yourself"

The near-universal opener. Don't tell your life story: give a 60–90 second summary — who you are professionally, your background in one thread, and what brings you here. End on the role you're targeting.

Trap: reciting your resume line by line. The recruiter has already read it. Tell the thread that connects your path instead.

2. "Why are you right for this role?"

Explicitly link your skills to the posting's needs. Take 2–3 key requirements from the ad and show how you meet each, with a concrete example.

3. "Why do you want to work here?"

Show you know the company (a project, a value, a product — real information). Connect what the company does to what motivates you. Avoid generic answers that could apply to any employer.

4. "What are your strengths?"

Pick 2–3 strengths relevant to the role, and prove each with an example. "I'm detail-oriented" means nothing without "for example, at X, I set up a process that cut errors by 30%."

5. "What's your biggest weakness?"

The classic trap. Don't give a fake weakness ("I'm too much of a perfectionist") — recruiters have heard it a thousand times. Give a real, minor weakness plus what you're doing to improve it. Honesty and self-awareness are what's being tested.

6. "Tell me about a challenge you overcame"

Use the STAR method: Situation (context), Task (your goal), Action (what YOU did), Result (the outcome, quantified if possible). This structure turns a vague anecdote into proof of competence.

7. "Where do you see yourself in 5 years?"

Show ambition aligned with the role and company. The recruiter wants to know if you'll stay and grow. Connect your projection to a possible path there — without making it sound like this job is just a stepping stone.

8. "Why are you leaving your current job?"

Stay positive. Never criticize your current employer — it's a red flag. Talk about what you're looking for (growth, a new challenge, alignment), not what you're running from.

9. "What are your salary expectations?"

Research market ranges for the role beforehand. Give a realistic range rather than a fixed number, and show flexibility. If possible, let the recruiter name their figure first.

10. "Do you have any questions?"

NEVER answer "no." Prepare 2–3 questions that show your interest: about the team, the role's challenges, how success is measured. This is also your moment to assess whether the company is right for you.

Mistakes that sink an interview

  1. Not researching the company — showing up knowing nothing about it is disqualifying.
  2. Reciting from memory — it sounds fake and robotic. Prepare ideas, not a script.
  3. Criticizing a former employer — the single biggest red flag for a recruiter.
  4. No concrete examples — "I'm motivated" means nothing without proof.
  5. Ignoring non-verbals (in person or video) — punctuality, eye contact, a smile, appropriate dress.
  6. Having no questions at the end — it signals a lack of interest.

Practice is the key to success

Reading these tips isn't enough — you have to practice out loud. Hearing yourself answer is how you spot hesitations and build fluency. Frequently asked questions

How do I prepare for a job interview?

Research the company, re-read the posting and your resume, prepare answers to common questions (tell me about yourself, your strengths, why this role), prepare concrete examples using the STAR method, and practice out loud. Also prepare 2–3 questions to ask the recruiter.

How do I answer "tell me about yourself"?

Give a 60–90 second summary: who you are professionally, the thread of your background, and what brings you to this role. Don't recite your resume — give its connecting thread and end on your motivation for the job.

What is the STAR method?

STAR (Situation, Task, Action, Result) is a structure for answering behavioral questions ("tell me about a challenge..."). You describe the context, your goal, what you concretely did, and the result. It turns an anecdote into proof of competence.

What questions should I ask the interviewer?

Prepare 2–3 questions about the team, the role's challenges, the priorities of the first months, or how success is measured. Avoid asking only about salary or time off first. Asking questions shows interest and helps you evaluate the company.
