The 12 Most Common Resume Mistakes (and How to Fix Them) in 2026

Your resume has seconds to convince. And often, it's not a lack of experience that eliminates you — it's an avoidable mistake: a typo, a layout that trips up screening software, one piece of information too many. The good news: these mistakes are easy to spot and fix once you know them.

Here are the 12 most common ones, grouped by type, with the fix for each.

Content mistakes

1. The generic resume, not tailored to the role

The number-one mistake. Sending the same resume to every job is the surest way to be ignored — by recruiters and by the ATS software that scans for the posting's keywords. Fix: at minimum, tailor your summary and skills to each posting, reusing its vocabulary.

2. Listing duties instead of achievements

"Responsible for inventory management" says nothing. "Cut stockouts by 30% by reorganizing tracking" proves your value. Fix: for each role, turn responsibilities into concrete, quantified results.

3. A vague or missing summary

"Seeking a challenging position" means nothing. Fix: 2–3 precise lines — who you are, your key experience, what you're targeting, with the role's keywords.

4. Too much unnecessary personal information

Marital status, religion, full ID number… none of it belongs, and it can even hurt you. Fix: keep it professional and essential. The photo itself is optional and should be removed for international applications.

Formatting mistakes

5. Spelling and grammar errors

The mistake that sinks a resume instantly. A typo signals a lack of care. Fix: proofread, use a checker, and have someone else read it too.

6. A cluttered or unreadable layout

Too many colors, fonts, and boxes: the recruiter gets lost and the ATS can't read anything. Fix: a clear structure, one or two legible fonts, white space. Restraint always looks more professional.

7. A resume that's too long

Beyond one page (two for experienced profiles), no one reads it all. Fix: cut the non-essential, keep the relevant. A concise resume is a read resume.

8. Missing, wrong, or unprofessional contact details

An email like "party_kid_92@...", an outdated number, or no LinkedIn: all missed opportunities. Fix: a professional email (firstname.lastname), a current phone number, an up-to-date LinkedIn.

Technical mistakes (that break ATS)

9. A format that breaks automatic screening

Tables, multiple columns, text boxes, icons instead of words: ATS software can't parse them and drops the resume. Fix: a single-column structure, standard section headings, real text (no images of text).

10. Sending the wrong file format

A resume as a .jpg, a screenshot, or a scanned PDF (an image) is unreadable by ATS. Fix: export as a PDF with selectable text (unless the posting asks for Word).

11. Missing the posting's keywords

ATS rank resumes by how well they match the posting. Without the right keywords, you rank poorly even with a strong profile. Fix: reuse the exact terms from the posting in your skills and summary.

The fundamental mistake

12. Not checking your resume before sending

Many people send without re-reading or testing. Fix: before every send, read it out loud, verify your contact details, and test ATS compatibility. You can check your resume for free in seconds: the tool flags structural problems, missing keywords, and gives you a score.

In summary

Most rejected resumes aren't rejected for a weak profile, but for one of these avoidable mistakes. Use this list as a checklist before every application: tailored and quantified content, clean and error-free form, ATS-compatible format, and a final check.

Frequently asked questions

What is the most serious resume mistake?

Spelling errors and a generic, untailored resume are the two most disqualifying mistakes. The first signals a lack of care, the second a lack of interest — and ATS drop resumes that miss the posting's keywords.

How many pages should a resume be?

One page for most profiles, two pages maximum for experienced candidates. A too-long resume isn't read in full; concise and relevant is better.

How do I know if my resume has technical errors?

Use a free ATS checker: it simulates automatic reading, flags structure and format problems, and lists missing keywords against a posting.

Should I put a photo on my resume?

It's optional and best avoided for international applications (anti-discrimination norms). On some local markets, a professional photo is still accepted. When in doubt, leave it off.
